The Common Mormon's Life Cycle
  • Pupae Stage

    The pupa stage, where the transformation occurs within the chrysalis, lasts for about 10 days . The pupa of Papilio polytes presents two color forms: green and brown. Green form pupae display a green coloration with yellowish diamond-like markings on the dorsal surface of the abdominal segment. Brown form pupae, on the other hand, are gray to dark brown . The pupa of this species is characterized by a pair of horns on its head, a hump on its back, and a tilted appearance.

  • Larvae Stage

    The larva stage, also known as the caterpillar stage, is characterized by rapid feeding and growth, lasting for approximately 14 days . The larvae initially appear yellowish-brown with dark brown lateral areas, measuring about 3 mm in length . As they progress to the fifth instar, their coloration transforms to a vibrant green, featuring an eye spot on the third thoracic segment connected by a transverse green band and a winding mark on their back.

  • Egg Stage

    The Common Mormon's life cycle begins with the egg stage, which typically lasts for 5 days . The eggs are laid singly on the top of leaves, appearing round and yellow to light-yellow in color.

    The Common Mormon (Papilio polytes), also known locally as "poletes," is a widespread swallowtail butterfly found throughout Asia. This species is renowned for its remarkable mimicry, particularly among its female forms. The Common Mormon exhibits a fascinating life cycle, transitioning through four distinct stages: egg, larva, pupa, and adult.

    Conservation Status +

    The Common Mormon is classified as Least Concern by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) . This designation indicates that the species is not currently facing significant threats to its survival.
